The claim being tested

One of the oldest ideas in the study of Congress is that committees are captured: that Agriculture is run by farm-state members, Armed Services by hawks and defense districts, the interior/lands committees by Western resource interests — each a cluster of preference outliers who don’t look like the chamber as a whole. That’s the distributive, “gains-from-trade” story (Shepsle–Weingast). Keith Krehbiel’s Are Committees Composed of Preference Outliers? (APSR 1990) put it to a direct test and largely found no: committees look like the floor.

This is a small, deliberately narrow, pre-registered replication of that specific measurable claim on modern data (1993–2022): do the committees distributive theory names sit measurably off the chamber median more often than chance, and more often than a control set?

NoteThis analysis was pre-registered

The hypothesis, the exact committee classification, the sample window, and the test were frozen in PRE_REGISTRATION.md before any committee–ideology join was run or seen. A result consistent with the null (committees mirror the chamber) was committed in advance to count as a finding, not a failure.

The headline result

H1 is not supported. The committees distributive theory says should be captured — Agriculture, Armed Services, Natural Resources / Energy, Transportation / Commerce — sit essentially on top of their chamber’s median. Their committee medians are displaced from the floor by only about 0.04–0.10 on the −1…+1 DW-NOMINATE scale — tiny. Across every committee and Congress in scope, the permutation test flags a significant median outlier only 0.8% of the time, below the 5% rate you’d expect by chance alone.

This is Krehbiel’s 1990 result, replicated on 2020s data: the committees everyone assumes are captured look like the chamber.

The test, exactly as pre-registered

For each committee × Congress: compute the committee median and the chamber median (DW-NOMINATE dim 1); run a two-sided, 10,000-draw permutation test — draw random chamber subsets equal in size to the committee, and flag the committee as a median outlier if its observed median falls outside the central 95% of the null distribution of random-subset medians; record signed displacement (committee − chamber).

The predicted-outlier set is a median outlier no more often than the control set (both ≈ 0.8%; the difference is +0.000, one-sided p = 0.76 — nowhere near supporting H1).

An honest limitation: the null is permissive

The one aggregate difference that is significant runs opposite to H1: the predicted (constituency) committees are, if anything, slightly more central than the control set (mean |displacement| 0.064 vs. 0.085; two-sided p = 0.005). This is not evidence against distributive theory — it is an artifact of two things that must be named plainly:

  1. The permutation null is conservative by construction. It draws unconstrained random subsets of the chamber. Real committees are built to roughly mirror the chamber’s party ratio; unconstrained random subsets can be far more party-lopsided than any real committee, which inflates the spread of the null distribution. A wider null makes real committee medians look central and pushes the outlier rate below the 5% chance floor. The pre-registered party-stratified null (sampling within party delegations) is the sharper instrument and is the planned v2; the “not outliers” conclusion here should be read as holding against a null deliberately tilted toward finding nothing.

  2. The significant gap is driven by the control set, not the treatment set. The largest displacement on the board belongs to House Rules (mean |displacement| 0.163), which sits in the not-predicted group. Rules is unrepresentative because party leadership packs it — a party-structural effect, not a constituency-demand one. Rules was not part of the pre-registered focus, so this is an exploratory observation, not a confirmatory finding; it happens to widen the control set’s average and thereby produce the opposite-signed significant difference.

What this does and doesn’t show

  • One statistic, one dimension. This tests the median on DW-NOMINATE dim 1 — the classic quantity. Committees can differ from the floor in variance or on issue-specific intensity without a median shift; that is out of scope for v1.
  • The null matters. As above, the unconstrained-random null is conservative; the party-stratified secondary (v2) is the sharper test and is pre-registered.
  • Exploratory vs. confirmatory. The Rules/leadership observation is exploratory. The confirmatory result is the pre-registered predicted-vs-not comparison, which does not support H1.
  • Coverage ends 2022 (117th) — the Stewart–Woon modern boundary.

The one-line read

The committees everyone assumes are captured by narrow interests look like the floor; the one committee that looks unrepresentative here is the one leadership controls — House Rules — an exploratory observation, not a tested claim. Krehbiel-consistent, with a modern twist — and a sharper party-stratified test still to come.
