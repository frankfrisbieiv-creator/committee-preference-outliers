perm_diff <- function(d, n_perm = N_PERM, seed = SEED) {
  set.seed(seed)
  pr <- d$group == "predicted"
  obs <- mean(d$outlier[pr]) - mean(d$outlier[!pr])
  n <- nrow(d); npred <- sum(pr)
  perm <- replicate(n_perm, { idx <- sample(n, npred)
    mean(d$outlier[idx]) - mean(d$outlier[-idx]) })
  list(rate_pred = mean(d$outlier[pr]), rate_not = mean(d$outlier[!pr]),
       diff = obs,
       p_1sided = (1 + sum(perm >= obs)) / (n_perm + 1),
       p_2sided = (1 + sum(abs(perm) >= abs(obs))) / (n_perm + 1))
}

The claim being tested

One of the oldest ideas in the study of Congress is that committees are captured: Agriculture run by farm-state members, Armed Services by hawks and defense districts, the interior/lands committees by Western resource interests, each a cluster of preference outliers who don’t look like the chamber as a whole. That’s the distributive, “gains-from-trade” story (Shepsle–Weingast). Keith Krehbiel’s Are Committees Composed of Preference Outliers? (APSR 1990) put it to a direct test and largely found no: committees look like the floor.

This is a small, deliberately narrow, pre-registered replication of that measurable claim on modern data (1993–2022): do the committees distributive theory names sit measurably off the chamber median more often than chance, and more often than a control set? The page walks the full arc: a first pass, a limitation I flagged in advance, and the sharper re-test I pre-committed to.

NoteThis analysis was pre-registered twice

The hypothesis, committee classification, sample window, and test were frozen in PRE_REGISTRATION.md before any committee–ideology join was run. The permissive null’s sharper successor (the party-stratified re-test below) was named in that same document (§7.3) and its exact construction frozen in PRE_REGISTRATION_v2.md before it was run. A result consistent with the null was committed in advance to count as a finding, not a failure.

First pass (v1): committees look like the chamber

The pre-registered test, for each committee × Congress: compute the committee median and the chamber median (DW-NOMINATE dim 1); run a two-sided, 10,000-draw permutation test: draw random chamber subsets equal in size to the committee, and flag the committee as a median outlier if its observed median falls outside the central 95% of the null distribution; record signed displacement (committee − chamber).

H1 was not supported. The committees distributive theory says should be captured sit essentially on top of their chamber’s median, displaced by only about 0.04–0.10 on the −1…+1 DW-NOMINATE scale. Across every committee and Congress in scope the test flags a median outlier only 0.8% of the time, below the 5% you’d expect by chance. The predicted set is an outlier no more often than the control set (difference +0.000, one-sided p = 0.76).

The limitation I flagged: that null is conservative

I pre-committed to naming a weakness in v1’s own terms. Its permutation null draws unconstrained random subsets of the chamber. But real committees are built to roughly mirror the chamber’s party ratio; unconstrained draws can be far more party-lopsided than any real committee, which inflates the spread of the null. A wider null makes real committee medians look central and pushes the outlier rate below the 5% floor, exactly the sub-chance rate seen above. So v1’s “not outliers” holds only against a null tilted toward finding nothing.

The tell was already in v1: the single largest displacement belongs to House Rules (mean |displacement| 0.163): it sits in the control group, unrepresentative because leadership packs it, a party-structural effect, not constituency demand. That is exactly the kind of party structure a party-blind null mishandles.

Sharper test (v2): hold party composition fixed

The pre-registered fix conditions on party. For each committee × Congress, v2 keeps the committee’s exact D/R counts and samples within each party from that chamber’s members of that party (10,000 draws), then takes the drawn committee’s overall median. Same sample, same classification, same gate: only the null is sharpened. If v1’s verdict were an artifact of a permissive null, this is where it breaks.

Confirmatory conclusion: H1 is still not supported

Tightening the null does not rescue the hypothesis. Under the party-stratified null the predicted-vs-not difference in overall-median outlier rate is +0.025 (one-sided p = 0.27, two-sided p = 0.54). The pre-registered confirmatory secondary (the within-party delegation test, pooled across parties) agrees: the predicted set’s within-party outlier rate exceeds the control set’s by only +0.061 (two-sided p = 0.062; one-sided p = 0.034). Reading the two-sided value as primary, that is marginal at best and does not clear 0.05: not evidence for H1.

The committees everyone calls captured look like their chamber at the median, under both the permissive and the sharp null.

Exploratory: a party-asymmetric sort hiding under a central median

ImportantThis section is exploratory, not confirmatory

Everything below is post-hoc, surfaced by the within-party results, not a pre-registered directional prediction. It is reported as exploratory, exactly as the v1 House Rules observation was.

A central overall median can mask structure: a committee’s two party delegations can each be pulled toward the extremes in ways that cancel. Split by party, the within-party effect is strong, and runs in opposite directions for the predicted and control sets:

Direction, not just rate. Signed displacement is the committee’s party-delegation median − that party’s chamber-wide median (negative = more liberal):

  • House Agriculture Democrats sit to the right of other House Democrats (+0.127) in all 15 Congresses.
  • House Armed Services Democrats likewise: +0.074, in 93% of Congresses.
  • On the control side, House Appropriations Republicans run the other way, more liberal than other Republicans (-0.087): a leadership/spending profile, not a constituency one.

The load-bearing caveat

WarningDW-NOMINATE dim 1 is general ideology, not issue-specific constituency demand

v2 sharpens the statistical null; it does nothing for the measurement gap. Dim 1 is a member’s overall roll-call liberal–conservative position. A committee can be genuinely constituency-captured (farm, defense, water) without its members being dim-1 extremists, and a within-party shift on dim 1 is consistent with constituency selection but does not establish it. “Agriculture Democrats are more conservative than other Democrats” is a fact about general ideology; it is suggestive of, but not proof of, a farm-district sort. Do not let “central at the median on dim 1” become “not captured,” and do not let “shifted within party on dim 1” become “captured.” Both overreads are wrong.

The one-line read

The committees everyone assumes are captured by narrow interests look like the floor at the median, and they still do once the null holds party composition fixed, so the pre-registered hypothesis is not supported under either test. What the sharper test surfaces, exploratorily, is a party-asymmetric within-party sort that cancels at the median: Agriculture and Armed Services Democrats sit to the right of their party in nearly every Congress. Consistent with constituency selection, but on general ideology, not issue demand, so it suggests rather than proves it. Krehbiel-consistent, with a modern twist.

::: {.callout-important}
## This section is exploratory, not confirmatory
Everything below is **post-hoc**, surfaced by the within-party results, not a pre-registered directional prediction. It is reported as exploratory, exactly as the v1 House Rules observation was.
:::

A central *overall* median can **mask** structure: a committee's two party delegations can each be pulled toward the extremes in ways that cancel. Split by party, the within-party effect is strong, and runs in **opposite directions** for the predicted and control sets:

```{r wp-byparty}
tibble(
  Party = c("Democrats", "Republicans"),
  `Predicted outlier rate` = round(c(wp_D$rate_pred, wp_R$rate_pred), 3),
  `Not-predicted outlier rate` = round(c(wp_D$rate_not, wp_R$rate_not), 3),
  `Δ (pred − not)` = round(c(wp_D$diff, wp_R$diff), 3),
  `p (2-sided)` = round(c(wp_D$p_2sided, wp_R$p_2sided), 4)
)
```

On the **Democratic** side the predicted committees' delegations are outliers far more often than the control set's (`r sprintf("%+.3f", wp_D$diff)`); on the **Republican** side it reverses (`r sprintf("%+.3f", wp_R$diff)`, driven by the control set). Pooled, these largely **cancel**, which is why the confirmatory pooled test is only marginal.

**Direction, not just rate.** Signed displacement is the committee's party-delegation median − that party's chamber-wide median (**negative = more liberal**):

- **House Agriculture Democrats** sit *to the right* of other House Democrats (**`r sprintf("%+.3f", d_ag)`**) in **all 15 Congresses**.
- **House Armed Services Democrats** likewise: **`r sprintf("%+.3f", d_as)`**, in `r sprintf("%.0f%%", 100*r_as)` of Congresses.
- On the control side, **House Appropriations Republicans** run the *other* way, *more liberal* than other Republicans (**`r sprintf("%+.3f", d_ap)`**): a leadership/spending profile, not a constituency one.

## The load-bearing caveat

::: {.callout-warning}
## DW-NOMINATE dim 1 is general ideology, not issue-specific constituency demand
v2 sharpens the **statistical null**; it does nothing for the **measurement** gap. Dim 1 is a member's overall roll-call liberal–conservative position. A committee can be genuinely constituency-captured (farm, defense, water) without its members being dim-1 extremists, and a within-party shift on dim 1 is *consistent with* constituency selection but does **not establish** it. "Agriculture Democrats are more conservative than other Democrats" is a fact about general ideology; it is suggestive of, but not proof of, a farm-district sort. Do not let "central at the median on dim 1" become "not captured," and do not let "shifted within party on dim 1" become "captured." Both overreads are wrong.
:::

## The gate still passes (both versions)

The party-switcher ICPSR crosswalk and the ≥98% join-completeness gate carry over from v1 unchanged; the pipeline halts on any shortfall. With the crosswalk applied, all `r nrow(comp)` chamber-Congress cells clear 98% (min `r sprintf("%.4f", min(comp$match_rate))`). The v1 aggregate is also identical whether the crosswalk is applied or the two affected Senate cells are dropped (Δ rate `r sprintf("%+.3f", agg_v1_b$d_rate)`), so three people cannot be driving anything.

## The one-line read

The committees everyone assumes are captured by narrow interests look like the floor at the median, and they *still* do once the null holds party composition fixed, so the pre-registered hypothesis is not supported under either test. What the sharper test surfaces, exploratorily, is a party-asymmetric within-party sort that cancels at the median: Agriculture and Armed Services Democrats sit to the right of their party in nearly every Congress. Consistent with constituency selection, but on general ideology, not issue demand, so it suggests rather than proves it. Krehbiel-consistent, with a modern twist.
